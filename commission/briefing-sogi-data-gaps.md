# What Washington Can't See
## A Commissioner Briefing on LGBTQ+ Data Gaps in State Systems

---

### The problem in one sentence

Washington cannot verify whether it is serving LGBTQ+ residents on the same terms as everyone else in foster care, adult homelessness, Medicaid, unemployment, or the basic-needs safety net — because the agencies running those systems do not collect sexual orientation and gender identity (SOGI) information at the front door.

### Why this matters

State agencies are responsible for delivering their services to all Washingtonians on the same terms. Without SOGI data, they cannot demonstrate whether they are doing so. National research consistently finds LGBTQ+ people over-represented in foster care, homelessness, behavioral health crises, and economic insecurity. Whether the same is true in Washington, and where, is not currently knowable from inside the state's own systems.

This is not a values question. It is a governance question. **You cannot evaluate program effectiveness for a population you cannot count.** Privacy is a design problem, not a reason not to build — Oregon has demonstrated that confidentiality and statewide collection are compatible.

---

### What Washington already does well

Washington has built real pieces of this infrastructure, and they are worth defending:

- **Hospitals report SOGI** in patient discharge data (since 2023).
- **The Healthy Youth Survey** — biennial, ~217,000 students — gives Washington one of the strongest data sources in the country on LGBTQ+ youth health and well-being.
- **The state's BRFSS health survey** includes SOGI questions, supporting population-level health estimates.
- **Apple Health** maintains records for trans members receiving gender-affirming care.
- **The Office of Equity** has statutory authority to set standards for disaggregated data collection.

Each of these is a genuine accomplishment. The problem is that they were built one at a time, in different statutes, with different question sets — and they leave large gaps.

---

### Where Washington is blind

These are the five state systems where the state currently cannot see how its LGBTQ+ residents are being served:

| System | What it does | What the state can't see |
|---|---|---|
| **DCYF FamLink** | Child welfare and foster care | Whether LGBTQ+ youth are over-represented in foster care, and how their placements compare |
| **Balance-of-State HMIS** | Adult homelessness services | How LGBTQ+ adults experience homelessness and shelter access |
| **Apple Health intake** | Medicaid enrollment | Whether LGBTQ+ Washingtonians face barriers getting and keeping coverage |
| **ESD Unemployment Insurance** | Job loss and re-employment | Whether LGBTQ+ workers face differential outcomes in the labor market |
| **DSHS ESA intake** | TANF, Basic Food, ABD | LGBTQ+ household economic vulnerability in the safety net |

The most urgent of these is child welfare. National research suggests LGBTQ+ youth are in foster care at roughly two to three times their share of the youth population, with even higher rates for trans youth. Washington has no way to confirm or refute that in its own system.

---

### What happens when the data shows a gap

The point of the data is not to add new programs. It is to give the state a way to see where its existing services are not landing equally — and to fix them. If foster care is producing unequal outcomes for LGBTQ+ youth, that is a foster care problem to solve. The data is the diagnostic, not the prescription.

---

### What Oregon did

In 2021, Oregon passed HB 3159. Three things to know about it:

1. **One standard.** A single set of questions on race, ethnicity, language, disability, sexual orientation, and gender identity — used across agencies.
2. **One confidential intake hub.** A centralized registry receives the data; only aggregate statistics are released publicly.
3. **One requirement.** Health providers, insurers, and Medicaid coordinated care organizations are all bound to the same standard.

Oregon also funded community organizations — tribal, BIPOC, LGBTQ+, disability, limited-English — to do the data work safely in their own communities.

The architectural insight: **Oregon built the plumbing first**, then turned on the requirement. Washington has built requirements without the plumbing.

---

### What the Commission can do

There are three pathways, in increasing order of ambition:

**1. Incremental — push for SOGI in one system at a time.** The most tractable starting point is DCYF FamLink, where the human stakes are highest and the system already supports demographic data capture. The Commission can advocate for this directly with DCYF and the legislature.

**2. Office-of-Equity-led standardization.** The Office of Equity already has the legal authority (RCW 43.06D) to publish a binding statewide data standard. This pathway achieves question-set unification without new legislation. It needs executive attention and budget support, not new law.

**3. Washington equivalent of Oregon's HB 3159.** A statute creating a centralized registry, a unified standard, binding requirements across state-funded programs, and dedicated funding. This is the structural fix. It needs a legislative champion and likely a multi-session timeline.

**The Commission's leverage is strongest in pathways 1 and 2.** Pathway 3 is the long arc.

The frame that holds all of this together: **Washington has built requirements. Oregon has built infrastructure.** Requirements without infrastructure produce fragmented data that cannot be combined or used at the population level. The gap is not ideological. It is architectural — and that means it is fixable.
