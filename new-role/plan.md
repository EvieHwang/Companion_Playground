The Plan

This document is a plan for Evie to get a new role. It explains the steps and objectives to accomplish in order to reach the goal. 

1. The framework, made durable **COMPLETE - see the file new-role/career-framework.md**
Why: Everything downstream depends on knowing what you’re solving for. The framework we built lives in this conversation; it needs to live somewhere portable so future AI conversations and your own re-reads can use it.
Contents: Role shape, domain thesis, floor criteria, target archetypes, two-cluster positioning logic, daily-texture model, your pattern of burn and the protection it requires.
Outcome: A single durable artifact (doc, Notion page, repo file) that captures the framework in your voice. Re-readable. Pasteable into any future AI conversation in 30 seconds.
2. Positioning narrative
Why: Every interaction — recruiter call, intro coffee, LinkedIn message, interview — opens with some version of “what are you looking for and why are you the right person for it.” If the answer isn’t pre-built and natural, you’ll deliver a different version each time and the signal degrades.
Contents: Two opening sentences (one for tech-flavored audiences, one for traditional/mission). A short “why I left Amazon” answer that’s honest without being bitter. A succinct “what I’m looking for next” that lands the Director-step-up without sounding like a stretch. A 30-second, 90-second, and full version.
Outcome: You deliver any version on demand, in writing or out loud, without drafting. Recruiters and warm contacts come away with a clear, repeatable image of who you are.
3. External presence
Why: Resume and LinkedIn are the surface every cold recruiter and target hiring manager sees first. They must reflect the Director-level positioning, not the Senior PM positioning. Your current resume is excellent for SPM roles and undersells you for the level you’re targeting.
Contents: A resume reframe that amplifies the value-stream-leader pattern, cross-functional architecture, and outcomes — not PM craft. LinkedIn headline and About aligned to positioning. Strategic visibility (a few posts or comments per month surfacing your thinking on operational leadership).
Outcome: Inbound recruiter activity at Director-level. Profile views from your target companies. Warm contacts can refer you to Director-level openings without hesitation.
4. Target list, operationalized
Why: Target companies need to become a working list, not memory. You need to know which roles are open at each, who the relevant leaders are, what their current operating challenges are, and which contacts you have or can develop.
Contents: Tier 1 (active interest + warm contact): ShipBob and any others where a path exists. Tier 2 (active interest, cold): Flexport, Auger, Costco, Microsoft Cloud Ops, Kaiser, REI, Patagonia, premium retail. Tier 3 (opportunistic): companies that emerge during discovery and fit the criteria.
Outcome: A living list — companies, contacts, current openings, status, intelligence notes. Updated weekly during active phase.
5. Network activation
Why: Warm intros are worth 100x cold applications because they get you into real conversations with decision-makers. Your existing network (Jen, Emily, GSBA, Commission, the ShipBob VP, Amazon and Nordstrom alumni, Slalom) is significant and largely untapped for this purpose. Activation also signals to people who care about you that you’re available — many opportunities surface through people who didn’t know you were looking.
Contents: A short, dignified note to your network announcing the transition and what you’re looking for. Targeted asks to specific people (ShipBob VP first). Discovery coffees with people in target domains — not asks for jobs, asks for landscape intel.
Outcome: A measurable cadence of meaningful conversations and warm intros. Companies on your list where you now have a path in.
6. Active application strategy
Why: Most cold applications go to a black hole. The active strategy is: warm intro > targeted application to a role where you have research-backed reason to apply > opportunistic application only when something surfaces that fits the framework. Volume is a trap; precision is what wins at Director-level.
Contents: A decision rule for each application — what makes it worth your time, what disqualifies it. A cover note template that references the framework. A practice of applying within 48 hours of a fit appearing, because timing matters.
Outcome: Application-to-conversation ratio. You’re having multiple substantive conversations per month. If the ratio is bad, the targeting is off.
7. Interview readiness
Why: At Director-level, the interview is less about credentials and more about leadership maturity, judgment, and fit with the existing team. The hardest questions will be: tell me about a time you led through ambiguity; what did you do at Amazon that you’re proudest of; why did you leave; what would you do in your first 90 days. These need to be pre-built and tested.
Contents: Five to seven core stories that demonstrate the value-stream-leader pattern. The honest, non-bitter version of why-Amazon-ended. A first-90-days hypothesis for each target company type. A practice partner — Jen or Emily ideally.
Outcome: You don’t dread interviews. You go in with stories ready, frameworks for hypotheticals, and the energy to be curious rather than performative.
8. Decision criteria, pre-built
Why: Decisions under pressure (offer in hand, deadline ticking) are systematically worse than decisions made in advance against pre-built criteria. Your floor is non-negotiable; the rest needs explicit weights so you can evaluate a real offer against the framework, not against the excitement of being wanted.
Contents: A scoring framework (or written rubric) covering scope and authority, team to lead, executive sponsor stability, comp, culture fit, mission fit, daily-texture match. Pre-written answers to “what would make me decline a $400k offer” and “what would make me accept a $250k offer.”
Outcome: When an offer arrives, you have a clear-headed assessment within 24 hours, not three weeks of agonizing.
9. Sustaining infrastructure
Why: Job search is emotional labor. The pattern we identified — your work being undervalued, your boundaries violated, your protection denied — can re-activate in a search where rejection is structural. Add the Commission work, your family, your Mandarin and French, the body practice. The search has to coexist with the life, not displace it.
Contents: A weekly rhythm — how many hours, when, where. Designated decompression. A check-in cadence with ShinShin or Katy about the search itself, not just logistics. A clear answer to “what would tell me I need to pause and reset?”
Outcome: You remain yourself through the search. Energy, sleep, relationship health stay intact. You don’t accept a bad role because you were tired of looking.

Top-level outcomes we’re tracking toward
	1.	Multiple credible offers, not a desperation-based single decision
	2.	A role that clears the floor and matches the framework — Director+, value stream leadership, operational flow domain, well-run company
	3.	A confirmed structural protection pattern — stable sponsor, clear scope, real authority — verified before signing
	4.	Comp in target range — strong preference for $300k+ total comp; lower acceptable only at exceptional structural fit
	5.	You enter the role rested and clear, not depleted and grateful
